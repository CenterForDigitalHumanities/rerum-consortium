The Rerum Consortium provides Digital Humanities software solutions for increased interoperability and access. Rerum tools and services are designed to ensure free and open sharing of data. Central to Rerum are OAC and IIIF as models for living digital objects for data interchange. The Rerum architecture facilitates the useful development of specific tools and interfaces without requiring scholars to have deep control over the underlying format.

The Story of Annotation

The Open Annotation Collaboration broke important ground on 08 February 2013, when they specified an interoperable framework for creating associations between related resources, annotations, using a methodology that conforms to the Architecture of the World Wide Web. The next year, SharedCanvas (later absorbed by IIIF), demonstrated how simply OAC could be extended to point to complex objects.

Pointing, instead of directly encoding annotations and annotation-like objects provides benefits for scholarship by separating the resource from the annotation, allowing for more granular and irregular resources, and creating a clearer history of the academic conversation. By separating the resource (text, image, etc.) from the annotation, the original digitized resource remains pristine and can be located anywhere in the world. Selectors remove the need for every reference to be to a complete resource while also allowing a scholar to make a very specific annotation on a smaller range of the original resource, whether or not it has native segmentation within it. Finally, every annotation can be individually attributed to the asserting scholar and software, which makes filtering and walking back through the revisions a simpler process.

Realizing Digital Resources

Early facsimiles, transcriptions, and photographs tended to either have very little connection to the real objects they depicted, or completely stood in for them as a digital replacement. The IIIF Presentation API clearly respects the role of the resource as a surrogate, while normalizing the digital containers for these resources.

The Rerum Consortium expands this effort by imagining digital containers beyond the Canvases and Manifests of IIIF to include useful objects for other media, such as audio, video, and three-dimensional models. In this way, advances in annotation standards can be applied to a wider set of resources and, importantly, these resources can be related to or described in terms of each other.

A Developing Need

Increasingly, scholars have expended time and money developing specialized programs or learning new programming languages in order to address their research questions successfully. Cataloguers who have already learned to speak a scholarly vocabulary and then describe objects with a carefully controlled schema are now being asked to understand the web ontology and data exchange formats. It would feel nonsensical to ask a librarian to establish the standard width for drawers in a card catalog or the height of a reference desk. Likewise, Digital Humanities should be offering tools that enforce standards by default and allow users to choose the motivation for the task. The conventional approach of encoding resources closer to the bare metal is fraught with misinterpretation and syntax errors.

Digital Humanities needs developers the way libraries need electricians and plumbers. Digital tools should support the standards of interoperability and digital transmission invisibly and responsibly enforce the scholarly standards for description, classification, and attribution. The best mind to address challenging research questions should not be required to also be the best mind in command-line programming, but work in concert with tools which accelerate analysis, automate encoding, and translate normal scholarly behavior into rich digital assets.
