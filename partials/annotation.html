Annotation

Annotations are the red blood cells of the academic conversation, bringing attention to those resources that are interesting, described, and about which much has been asserted. There should be no boundaries in their generation, encoding, or storage.

Generating Annotations

Designers make tools for scholars. A programmer will transform user inputs into encoded data, but when a scholar is asked to wear a programmer hat to understand a DH tool, she is dissuaded from thinking like herself. The design of an interface for each task may reuse logical modules, but design moves it from usable to useful.

The best tools will make important interface choices for the user, rendering views that respect the motivation of the annotation and the intelligence of the user. In creating a tool for annotation, there are considerations of function, intention, accessibility, and attribution.

Not all annotations are annotations. A scholar who will easily recognize a comment or edit as an annotation may not describe a transcription or metadata in the same way. Almost certainly the word "annotation" is inappropriate in the interface for a oa:FragmentSelector. The designer must be careful to suppress problematic terms for the user without concealing their existence.

Not everything is important. Respect the scope of the tool you are creating when determining what to allow. A text annotation tool does not need to expose image annotations or irrelevant aggregations, even if it retrieves them from the graph. In some cases, new annotations may be limited to few types, even while editing existing annotations may not be as limited. While every annotation should record its provenance, it is unreasonable to visibly cite every string on screen.

Encoding Annotations

First, do no harm. Unencoded data runs the risk of isolating itself, but poorly encoded data loose in the wild can contaminate large datasets and degrade confidence in the hosting institution. Please recognize that the data model encoding the content of a resource is a separate concern from the encoding format and model for storing data. This consortium expects text-based annotations to be deliverable as JSON-LD, but recognizes that other text formats, such as TTL and XML are often used.

Storing Annotations

Finders, keepers. Anyone who wants to can host open data. The beauty of it is that because each annotation is attributed individually, there are very few concerns (aside from vandalism) for mirroring unreliable stores or offering a broad service. While it may be simplest to use the RERUM store, it will not fit every project.
